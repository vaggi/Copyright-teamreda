Reform of Copyright Law in the Europaen Parliament (Report by Julia Reda, Pirate Party)

European Parliament: the Reda Report

The Reform

The 2001 Directive on copyright and related rights was adopted in order to make these rights fit for the digital era.
However, in actuality this directive blocks the free exchange of information and culture between European countries.

Would you like to know more?

The Reform

By following the recommendations of Julia Reda's report, Europe can develop a copyright law which promotes creativity.
Copyright law should not be content with protecting older works, but must primarily encourage the creation of new works and establish a common European market for culture.

At the end of 2013, the EU initiated a public consultation on the reform of copyright law and related rights. To everyone's surprise, this consultation registered one of the highest levels of participation ever. So many people wanted to participate that the Commission had to extend the consultation period. By the end, more than 11,000 people and organizations had given their views.

The individual steps:

The reform is a long democratic process.

Dec. 2013 to March 2014: Copyright consultation

More than 11,000 people and organizations have given their views.

20th January 2015: Presentation

Presentation of the draft report before the Committee on Legal Affairs European Parliament (JURI)

23rd - 24th February 2015: Committee on Legal Affairs (JURI)

Debate in the Committee on Legal Affairs (will be broadcasted live)

3rd March 2015: Deadline for Amendments

Any changes will be announced on this day.

23rd/24th March 2015: Discussion of amendments in the Committee on Legal Affairs (JURI)

25th March 2015: Vote in the Committee on Internal Market and Consumer Protection (IMCO)

Vote on the opinion on the draft report in the Committee on Internal Market and Consumer Protection. The opinion of IMCO-committee is then submitted to the committee responsible for drafting the report, which in this case is JURI.

14th April 2015: Vote in the Committee on Industry, Research and Energy (ITRE)

Vote on the opinion on the draft report in the Committee on Industry, Research and Energy. The opinion of ITRE-committee is then submitted to the committee responsible for drafting the report, which in this case is JURI.

16th April 2015: Vote in the Committee on Culture and Education (CULT)

Vote on the opinion on the draft report in the Committee on Culture and Education. The opinion of CULT-committee is then submitted to the committee responsible for drafting the report, which in this case is JURI.

06th May 2015: Release of the Digital Single Market strategy

Vice President of the European Commission and Commissioner for the Digital Single Market, Andrus Ansip, releases the Digital Single Market strategy, which encompasses copyright reform.

16th June 2015: Final vote in the Committee on Legal Affairs (JURI)

The draft report will be voted upon in the Committee on Legal Affairs taking into account the opinions provided by ITRE and CULT. The final report will then be submitted to the plenum of the European Parliament.

08th/09th July 2015: Final vote in plenary session

The final report will be voted upon during the plenary session of the European Parliament.

Duration of protection of a work

Harmonization of copyright term to 50 years following the death of the author

Paragraph 7 of the draft report: Calls on the Commission to harmonise the term of protection of copyright to a duration that does not exceed the current international standards set out in the Berne Convention.
Explanation: At present there is no harmonization at European level: each state can determine the length of its own copyright term. The result is Europe-wide confusion: a work that is protected in one country can be freely available in another.
Example: The works of Saint-Exupéry have been in the public domain since January 1st throughout the EU, with the exception of France, where the text is protected until 2033. The copyright term for works by French authors with the status »Mort pour la France« (died in service to France) is 30 years longer than the normal term. As a result, a copy of The Little Prince made in Brussels, though fully legal there, would be illegal in Strasbourg.

The vast majority of end users consider that the current terms of copyright protection are inappropriate. … Institutional users generally believe that the current terms […] should be shortened. ... Some broadcasters [...] think that the term is too long ... The vast majority of intermediaries and service providers consider that the term of protection is too long.
— European Commission, copyright consultation report (.pdf, p. 27 et seq.)

Positive macroeconomic effects can only be documented for short protection terms of below about 30 years.
— Gutachten 2015, Expert Commission for Research and Innovation, instated by the German government (.pdf, p. 61)

Digital borrowing rights

Bridging the digital divide in libraries.

Paragraph 20 of the draft report: Calls for the adoption of a mandatory exception allowing libraries to lend books to the public in digital formats, irrespective of the place of access.
Explanation: Libraries are an important force for the sharing and distribution of culture at low costs. The digitalization of works should not lead to the disappearance of this cornerstone of education and transmission of knowledge and information.
According to an investigation by the organisation Shelf Free conducted in February 2013 85% of ebooks were not available in public libraries.

The current legal framework hinders libraries from fulfilling [their] essential services for our society in the digital era.
— EBLIDA: European libraries and the challenges of e‐publishing

Research and education

Establishment of a general exception for education and research institutions

Paragraph 9 of the draft report: Notes that exceptions and limitations in the digital environment should be enjoyed without any unequal treatment as compared with those granted in the analogue world.
Paragraph 18: Stresses the need to enable automated analytical techniques for text and data (e.g. ‘text and data mining’) for all purposes, provided that permission to read the work has been acquired.
Paragraph 19: Calls for a broad exception for research and education purposes, which should cover not only educational establishments but any kind of educational or research activity, including non-formal education.
Explanation: Julia Reda's report again proposes harmonization in the areas of research and education. At present, each member state can use its own judgement to choose whether to recognize each exception or not. Often the exceptions vary considerably between member states, for instance with regard to permission to create digital copies of texts. The report also comes out in favour of an exception for the increasingly-used technology of data mining.

A specific and mandatory exception to remove text and data mining for scientific purposes from the reach of European copyright and database law should be considered.
— EC Text and Data Mining expert group recommendation (.pdf, S. 7)

Example: Since teachers are not allowed to distribute digital copies of texts that are relevant to their teaching (for instance across a school intranet), they waste a substantial proportion of their time making physical (dead wood) copies.
Such restrictions also make it more difficult for European universities to offer online courses (MOOCs), which are becoming increasingly popular.

Digital Rights Management (DRM)

The use of legal exceptions to copyright must be guaranteed
Facilitation of the interplay of varying technical protection measures (DRM)

Paragraph 23 of the draft report: Stresses that the effective exercise of exceptions or limitations, and access to content that is not subject to copyright or related rights protection, should not be hindered by technological measures.
Paragraph 24: Recommends making legal protection against the circumvention of any effective technological measures conditional upon the publication of the source code or the interface specification, in order to secure the integrity of devices on which technological protections are employed and to ease interoperability; considers, in particular, that where the circumvention of technological measures is allowed, technological means to achieve such authorised circumvention must be available.
Explanation: Technical protection measures (DRM) are acceptable under the following conditions:

  • They must not prevent the end user from exercising their rights (i.e. acknowledging exceptions such as making legal private copies).

  • They are only permitted when the source code is publicly accessible (open source) and can therefore be examined to check whether a back door has been built in or whether there are other security risks for the computer of the end user.

Example: DRM mechanisms frequently prevent end users from exercising their right to produce a backup copy of a DVD they have bought, even though when purchasing this DVD they have paid a fee that entitles them to this right (the so-called private copying levy).

Unified Europe-wide regulations

An end to the territorial fragmentation of European copyright lawmaking

Paragraph 4 of the draft report: Considers the introduction of a single European Copyright Title on the basis of Article 118 TFEU that would apply directly and uniformly across the EU, in accordance with the Commission’s objective of better regulation, as a legal means to remedy the lack of harmonisation resulting from Directive 2001/29/EC.
Explanation: The old Directive 2001/29/EG contains 21 exceptions and restrictions, which each member state can choose whether to implement or not. This yields 2,097,152 possibilities for implementing the directive.


The vast majority of end users/consumers consider that the EU should pursue [this] idea [which] would enhance legal certainty and transparency for right owners and consumers [and] reduce transaction and licensing costs related to the clearance of rights [...] The vast majority of institutional users are also generally in favour [as well as] a significant number of authors/performers.
— European Commission, copyright consultation report (.pdf, p. 6, 89 et seq.)

See also the open letter written by the European Copyright Society to Commissioner for Digital Economy and Society Günther Oettinger regarding the »unification of copyright law«.

No copyright protection for public works

Works produced by the public sector are exempted from copyright protection

Most pictures by US-space agency NASA are in the public domain, whereas those of her European counterpart ESA are not.

Paragraph 5 of the draft report: Recommends that the EU legislator further lower the barriers to the re-use of public sector information by exempting works produced by the public sector – as part of the political, legal and administrative process – from copyright protection.
Explanation: Works produced by employees of governments, public administration and courts in the course of their duties should be made exempt from copyright protection. This serves, among other things, to prevent governments from misusing copyright law in order to make it difficult for the public to access information.
Example: In 2014 the German Federal Ministry of the Interior tried to use copyright law to prevent access to documents (de) that a citizen had previously obtained via a freedom of information request. The Ministry lost the court case.
A year earlier, the Federal Ministry of Defence had also tried (de) to use copyright law to prevent the publication of documents.

Increased legal protection for authors

The EU should do more to protect the rights of authors

Paragraph 3 of the draft report: Acknowledges the necessity for authors and performers to be provided with legal Protection for their creative and artistic work; recognises the role of producers and publishers in bringing works to the market, and the need for appropriate remuneration for all categories of rightholders; calls for improvements to the contractual position of authors and performers in relation to other rightholders and intermediaries.
Explanation: One of the biggest challenges for authors in a rapidly changing online environment is to negotiate fair contracts with rights-holders and intermediaries. The European Union should do more to protect the rights of creative workers.

A decisive siding with creatives which [...] can only be supported from the creators’ side.
— German artist coalition Initiative Urheberrecht on the report

Most artists seeking to earn a living from artistic expressions must negotiate copyright licences with corporations to commercialize their works. Those contractual exchanges are often marked by an imbalance of power between the parties. Corporations may leverage a stronger bargaining position to retain most of the resulting profit, reducing benefits for artists. Copyright policy can help protect authors from such vulnerability. [...]
States should protect artists from exploitation in the context of copyright licensing and royalty collection [...] through legal protections that may not be waived by contract.
— United Nations, Copyright policy and the right to science and culture, Farida Shaheed (.doc, p. 10 et seq.)

Concern has been expressed about coercive contracts that authors and artists identify as a primary obstacle to fair remuneration. Under such contracts, which are frequent, creators sign away all their rights to their creation in order to gain a commission for creating a work. Consequently, they lose control over their creation, which can be used in contradiction to their own vision. [...]
On all these issues, artists are reluctant to enter into lengthy and costly judicial proceedings against corporations, which in turn can be a deterrent to artistic creativity.
— United Nations, Report of the Special Rapporteur in the field of cultural rights, Farida Shaheed (.pdf, p. 17 et seq.)

Future-proof open norm

Creation of an open norm on the basis of the Three-Step Test

Paragraph 13 of the draft report: Calls for the adoption of an open norm introducing flexibility in the interpretation of exceptions and limitations in certain special cases that do not conflict with the normal exploitation of the work and do not unreasonably prejudice the legitimate interests of the author or rightholder.

Explanation: The reform of European copyright law takes time. Fourteen years have passed between the introduction of the original Copyright Directive and its evaluation. Since the old Directive refers to outmoded technologies such as the CD-ROM, it is likely that future technological developments will outpace changes in legislation. An open norm would allow courts to permit future usage scenarios on the basis of the Three-Step Test:

1. A special use of a work...
2. ...which does not conflict with a normal exploitation of the work...
3. ...and does not unreasonably prejudice the legitimate interests of the author.

This open norm differs substantially from the US notion of "Fair Use", since it exists alongside a number of exceptions and restrictions and does not replace these. It is a circumvention mechanism that equips exceptions and restrictions with the necessary flexibility and makes them future-proof.

The vast majority of institutional users favour increasing the flexibility of exceptions in EU copyright law, and most often see the solution in the introduction of [an] open norm, in addition to the list of specific limitations. ... Many consumers suggest adding an open norm to the current list of exceptions to permit uses that could not be foreseen at the time of the adoption of the legislation. ... some [Member States] consider that some of the existing exceptions are not technology neutral hence an extension to ‘‘‘similar uses’’’ or another type of open norm could be considered. ... A small fraction of authors and collective management organisations argue that making the list of exceptions open-ended could help EU law keep pace with technological change. ... Other [academics] argue that adding an open norm to the existing list of exceptions would be the best means to ensure that the relevant legislation is future-proof.
— European Commission, copyright consultation report (.pdf, p. 33 et seq.)
